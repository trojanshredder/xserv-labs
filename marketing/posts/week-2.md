## Monday — Founder personal

**Topic:** The compliance check nobody runs
**Account:** Founder personal

```
Came across a client this month who had been operating a financial product for three years without a single one of the security checks their license required them to run.

They were not bad people. Nobody had done anything malicious. The compliance audit had been "scheduled" every year and quietly pushed to next quarter every quarter. Then a routine inspection happened, and now they are scrambling.

The pattern is universal. Compliance work in small companies is the easiest thing to defer because nothing visible breaks when you skip it. Until something does. Then everything breaks at once.

If you run a business with any kind of regulatory layer, the move is to bake the checks into the calendar as recurring infrastructure tasks owned by an actual person. Not "we will get to it." A name, a deadline, a checklist that gets ticked or escalated.

The cost of doing this is small. The cost of not doing it is sometimes the company.

## Tuesday — Engineering perspective

**Topic:** Cursor / AI coding tools after a month of real use
**Account:** Founder personal (engineer voice)

```
We have been running our team on Cursor (AI code editor) for about a month now. Honest review:

The good. Boilerplate is genuinely faster. New endpoints take half the time. Reading unfamiliar codebases is faster because you can ask the assistant to walk you through them. Onboarding a new engineer last week was noticeably easier than the previous hire six months ago.

The neutral. Code review is the same workload. The AI does not catch the things human reviewers catch. It catches a different set of things.

The bad. Junior engineers ship more code now and have a harder time understanding what they shipped. This is not Cursor's fault, but it is something we have had to compensate for with more pairing time, more verbal walkthroughs, and explicit "explain this without using the tool" sessions.

Net: keeping it. The productivity gain is real. The hidden cost is the discipline of making sure people are still learning the craft rather than driving the tool.

If you are evaluating, do not let your seniors decide alone. Watch what the juniors do with it for two weeks before you commit.

#xservlabs #ai
```

## Thursday — Traxium product

**Topic:** Document expiry feature — the small one that saves real money
**Account:** Traxium company

```
The least sexy feature in Traxium is the document expiry tracker. It is also probably the highest ROI for our customers.

Trucks have a lot of paperwork. Registration certificates. Permits. Fitness certificates. PUC. Insurance. National permits. State permits for whichever states the truck runs in. Every one of these has an expiry date. Every one of them, when expired, can mean a truck sitting at a checkpoint for hours, a fine, sometimes a load that misses its window.

Most fleet operators we work with were tracking these on a spreadsheet that they remembered to look at sometimes. Or in their head, which is worse. Or in the driver's head, which is worst.

Traxium reads the document data on setup, then pings the office on WhatsApp at 30 days, 14 days, and 7 days before any expiry. Three customers have told us specifically that this feature alone has paid for the product, multiple times, in fines avoided.

If you run a fleet and your document calendar is "we usually remember," try the free month.

## Friday — Founder personal

**Topic:** On not hiring the obvious candidate
**Account:** Founder personal

```
Best hire we made last year was a candidate we almost did not interview.

His resume was light. He had two years of experience at a company nobody had heard of. No GitHub presence. No blog. No referral. He applied via the contact form on our website with a one-paragraph cover letter that said roughly "I think I would be useful to you, can we talk."

We talked. Twenty minutes in I had a screen-share open and I was asking him to debug something I had pulled from one of our actual codebases. Forty minutes in I had texted my cofounder asking if we could move fast on this one.

The skill the interview surfaced was not technical brilliance. It was patience. He read the code slowly, asked questions, did not rush to a conclusion, said "I do not know" twice before he was sure of the answer, then he was right.

A lot of the time we hire for the version of the engineer who interviews well. Cracks the puzzle. Talks fast. Charismatic. Those engineers exist and some are great. But the engineers who stay great over years are the patient ones who do not need to be the smartest person in the room.

If you are hiring, ignore the resume for thirty minutes. Watch how they think when they don't know the answer.

#xservlabs #hiring
```
