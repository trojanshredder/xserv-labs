## Monday — Founder personal

**Topic:** What I look for in a client before saying yes
**Account:** Founder personal

```
After signing the wrong client a few times in the early years, I have a short list now of things I check before agreeing to take a project on.

Not credit check things. Behavioural ones.

How they responded to our first email. Was it from a person, signed with a name, written like someone who reads emails? Or was it a forwarded request with no context?

How they paid the discovery deposit. On time, no questions, signal of respect. Late, with friction, signal of how the next eighteen months will go.

How they talked about their previous vendor. Were they specific and fair about what did not work? Or did they badmouth everyone they had ever worked with? The way someone talks about their last vendor is the way they will eventually talk about you.

How many people from their side were on the kickoff call. One person with the authority to decide and the people doing the work? Good. Six people, none of whom can decide anything? You are about to build for a committee.

Whether they read the proposal. We send proposals with specific technical detail. The clients who quote our own language back to us in a question are reading. The clients who say "looks great, when can you start" did not read it and will be confused about scope by month two.

None of this is foolproof. Some great clients had bad initial signals. Some bad clients had great ones. But the ratio is far enough that I trust the patterns more than the gut now.

```

## Tuesday — Engineering perspective

**Topic:** Why we write less code than we used to
**Account:** Founder personal (engineer voice)

```
The team is shipping more software this year than last year and writing less code to do it.

Some of this is AI tooling. Some of it is better libraries. Most of it is something quieter. We have gotten better at not writing the second feature.

When a client asks for a feature, the first version of the answer used to be "yes, we can build that." The second version is usually "do you actually need this, or do you need the thing this is a workaround for." About a third of the time the conversation reveals that the requested feature is unnecessary. We end up building something smaller, more correct, in less time.

This requires the engineer to be in the conversation. A project manager translating between client and team does not have the context to do this. They write down what was asked for and we build it.

The engineers who get good at this conversation become 3-4x more valuable than ones who do not. Not because they code faster. Because they delete features that should not exist.

If you are running an engineering team, the metric to optimise for is not lines of code per week. It is features avoided per month. The number is harder to measure. The impact compounds for years.

#xservlabs #softwaredevelopment
```

## Thursday — Traxium product

**Topic:** What the Traxium analytics tab actually shows you
**Account:** Traxium company

```
The analytics tab in Traxium was not in the original plan. We added it because customers kept asking the same questions and we got tired of pulling the data manually for them.

What it shows now:

- Trips per vehicle per week, month, quarter
- TAT exceedance by route, by customer, by vehicle, by driver
- Fuel cost per kilometre, broken down by vehicle and by lane
- Idle hours by vehicle (the silent expense most operators do not track)
- Loading and unloading delays by customer (helps when you negotiate next year's rates)
- Revenue per vehicle per month, by customer

The thing customers are mostly using it for is the customer-level analysis. Most operators have a feeling about which clients are profitable and which are not. The data almost always surprises them. A customer that pays well but causes long unloading delays is usually less profitable than the one paying less for fast turnarounds.

If you operate a fleet and you have never broken your customer P&L down by actual TAT and idle hours, the analytics tab will pay for itself the first time you look at it.
```

## Friday — Founder personal

**Topic:** On building a company that runs without you
**Account:** Founder personal

```
Took six days off this month. Did not check Slack. Did not check email. Phone in another room for most of it.

The company ran fine. Two minor things came up that needed me when I got back, neither was urgent. Everything else was handled.

This is the result of three or four years of deliberate work. Not a personality trait. Not a lucky team. A set of specific choices, made on purpose, that reduce the number of decisions that have to flow through me.

The choices, briefly:
- Hire people who can make decisions, not people who can execute on decisions
- Pay them enough that they are not constantly looking elsewhere
- Document the principles, not the procedures (procedures change, principles guide)
- Resist the urge to weigh in on every decision in the team's Slack
- Be unavailable sometimes on purpose so the team builds the muscle of resolving things without you

The trap is that for the first few years of a company you actually do need to be in everything. The shift to not-being-in-everything is uncomfortable because it looks like you are doing less. You are. That is the point.

If you can take six days off and your business runs fine, you are not lucky. You have built something that scales. If you cannot, that is fixable, but the fix is mostly about you, not about your team.

#xservlabs #founderlife
```
